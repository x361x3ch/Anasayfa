For 38 years, the US paid farmers NOT to grow too much corn. In 1971, one guy killed that rule. Within 13 years your Coke had corn syrup instead of sugar, food was the cheapest it had ever been, and Americans were getting heavier every year. Since the Great Depression, US farm policy ran on simple supply and demand. If everyone planted everything, prices would crash and farmers would go broke. So the government paid farmers to leave a chunk of their land empty, and held big stockpiles of grain like an emergency fund. Then Nixon picked Earl Butz to run the Department of Agriculture. Butz was a farm-economy professor from Indiana who also sat on the boards of giant food companies. He told farmers to "get big or get out" and to plant every inch of land they owned. In 1972, when the Soviet Union had a bad harvest and came shopping, Butz quietly sold them 30 million tons of grain in one deal. The US emergency stockpile was gone overnight. By 1976 he had killed the entire 38-year-old system. By the late 1970s, the country was drowning in corn, and Washington kept guaranteeing the prices anyway. Corn became the cheapest ingredient in the American grocery store. The government still hands corn farmers about 3. 2 billion dollars a year, more than any other crop. That cheap corn went two places. The first was your soda. Scientists had recently figured out how to turn corn starch into a syrup that tasted almost like sugar. With corn this cheap, that syrup (high-fructose corn syrup, or HFCS) was way cheaper than cane sugar. Coca-Cola started swapping it in by 1980. By 1984, Coke and Pepsi had ditched cane sugar entirely in the US. The average American went from eating zero corn syrup in 1970 to almost 38 pounds of it a year by 1999. The second place was everything else. That same cheap corn fed the cows, pigs, and chickens packed into industrial farms. It also became the base ingredient or sweetener in most processed food on the shelf. Americans went from spending 17 percent of their take-home pay on food in 1960 to under 10 percent by 2000, one of the lowest rates in the world. Daily calories per person climbed from about 2, 054 in 1970 to over 2, 500 by 2010. The extra 500 came mostly from added fats, refined grains, and corn syrup. When Butz took office in 1971, about 15 percent of American adults were obese; today the CDC says it's 40. 3 percent. Severely obese, defined as way past overweight, used to be under 1 percent. Now it's nearly 1 in 10. Butz's policy did exactly what it promised. Productivity, exports, and grocery prices all moved the way he said they would, year after year for three decades. The right photo is just what happens to the average American body after fifty years of policy designed to make calories as cheap as possible.

Part 2. Your fast-food meal today is 4x bigger than the same meal in 1955. The single business decision that made it happen came from a movie theater executive who couldn't get his customers to buy two bags of popcorn. In the late 1950s, David Wallerstein ran the snack counter for Balaban & Katz, the biggest movie theater chain in Chicago. His one job was to sell more popcorn. He tried matinee discounts, two-for-one deals, free refills. Nothing worked. Customers bought one bag and stopped. So Wallerstein started watching them. He saw people drain their drinks dry and shake popcorn bags for the last bits. They clearly wanted more, but wouldn't buy a second bag. Wallerstein figured out why: buying two bags of popcorn made people look like a pig in front of strangers. His fix was simple. He kept the same base price but introduced a single bigger bag and a giant cup. Now buying "the large" delivered the same total food as two bags would have, with no social shame. Sales recovered overnight. In 1968 he joined the board of McDonald's and pitched Ray Kroc on the same trick for fries and Cokes. Kroc was unconvinced, arguing that customers who wanted more food could just order a second burger. So Wallerstein staked out McDonald's parking lots in Chicago. He watched customers chug their sodas, shake fries bags upside down for crumbs, and rip ketchup packets open for the last drops. He brought the data back, and Kroc relented. The "Large" fries showed up first. Then the "Large" Coke. By 1987 McDonald's was running a "Big Mac, Large Fries, Large Coke" summer promo. By 1994 it had a name: "Super Size. " The numbers tell the rest. McDonald's opened in 1955 with one size of everything: fries 2. 4 ounces, Coke 7 ounces, burger 3. 7 ounces. Today the "small" fries weighs 2. 6 ounces and the "large" weighs 5. 9, almost two and a half times the original. A large Coke is 30 ounces, four times bigger. The Big Mac is 7. 5 ounces. The average American restaurant meal today is 4 times the size of the same meal in the 1950s, per the CDC. Wallerstein had stumbled onto something every food company now treats as a textbook fact. Human appetite is elastic. A 2002 Penn State study from Barbara Rolls served people different portions of mac and cheese on different days. Given a portion twice the size, they ate 30% more, and reported feeling no fuller. Humans evolved to eat whatever is in front of them, and the body's fullness signals never catch up in time. Wallerstein died in 1993, right before the Super Size button went on the menu boards. People will eat almost any amount of food in front of them, as long as it shows up in one container. The right photo is the photograph of a country that has spent 60 years buying the largest single serving available.

Part 3. In 1965, the sugar industry paid Harvard scientists $6, 500 to publish a study blaming fat for heart disease. That single paper shaped 50 years of American eating, and is part of why the photos at the top look the way they do. In the early 1960s, scientific evidence was starting to point at sugar. Research suggested sugar consumption raised the risk of heart disease. The Sugar Research Foundation, the sugar industry’s trade group, decided to fight back before the science hardened. Its public relations head, John Hickson, recruited Frederick Stare, the chair of Harvard’s nutrition department, onto the foundation’s scientific board. Hickson then offered Stare’s colleagues, D. Mark Hegsted and Robert McGandy, $6, 500 to write a literature review of the heart disease research. The catch: they would only review the studies the foundation handed them. Hegsted agreed. The review came out in two parts in the New England Journal of Medicine in August 1967, under Stare, Hegsted, and McGandy’s names. The conclusion: the only dietary change needed to prevent heart disease was less fat and cholesterol. Sugar’s role was “limited. ” At the time, NEJM did not require conflict-of-interest disclosure, and the sugar industry’s role would stay hidden for decades. In 1977, that same Mark Hegsted became the lead scientific adviser to Senator George McGovern’s nutrition committee. The committee’s report, “Dietary Goals for the United States, ” was the first time the federal government officially told Americans what to eat. The advice: cut fat, cut cholesterol, push carbs up to 55-60% of calories. The 1980 US Dietary Guidelines copied it. By 1992, the Food Pyramid put 6-11 servings of bread, rice, and pasta at its base in every American classroom. Food companies adapted overnight. Fat got pulled out of nearly every product on the shelf, replaced with sugar to keep the food palatable. Snackwell’s, Diet Coke, low-fat yogurt, low-fat granola, low-fat ice cream. People ate more of these products because the label said “low-fat, ” and the sugar inside drove appetite higher. In 2016, a UCSF dentist named Cristin Kearns went looking for the smoking gun. She found 2, 000 pages of internal Sugar Research Foundation correspondence in a Harvard basement archive. The cache included Hickson’s letters to Hegsted, the article list the foundation sent, drafts of the review with the foundation’s edits. Kearns published the documentation in JAMA Internal Medicine on September 12, 2016. The Sugar Association’s response was muted. They acknowledged the foundation had not exercised greater transparency, but argued disclosure standards in 1965 were different from today’s. In today’s dollars, that 1967 bribe was about $65, 000. That is what redirected American food advice for half a century, and helped engineer the slow-motion swap of fat for sugar in the American diet. By 1999, the average American consumed 152 pounds of sugar per year. That was the highest in recorded human history. The right photo is what happens when the country spends sixty years fearing the wrong food.

Part 4. American workers burn 142 fewer calories per day at their jobs than they did in 1960. That single change, with no other diet or lifestyle assumptions, predicts almost all the weight America gained over the next fifty years. In 2011, a team of researchers at the Pennington Biomedical Research Center at Louisiana State University, led by Timothy Church, set out to answer one question: how much of the obesity epidemic could be explained by what Americans did at work, before food intake came into the picture. They pulled fifty years of US Bureau of Labor Statistics data on every job category in the country. In the early 1960s, almost half of all private-sector US jobs required at least moderate physical activity. Construction workers, factory line workers, mechanics, dock workers, farmers, miners, machinists. By 2008, fewer than 20% of those jobs remained, replaced by service work that mostly required sitting still. The American economy had moved out of the warehouse and onto the office chair. The team estimated daily calorie burn for each job, weighted by how many Americans held each job, and calculated the population shift. Compared to 1960, the average male private-sector worker today burns 142 fewer calories per day at the office. For women, the number was 124 calories. Then they did something useful with those numbers. They plugged them into a basic energy balance model and asked what the workplace shift alone, with no change to food intake or anything else, would predict for American body weight. The average man in 1960-62 weighed 76. 9 kg, about 169 pounds; the model said he should have grown to 89. 7 kg, about 198 pounds, by 2003-06. The actual measurement from federal health surveys for that same period: 91. 8 kg, about 202 pounds. The model came within 4 pounds. Roughly 90% of the average male’s weight gain over those decades was explained by the workplace alone, before any change in food intake. The gym after work cannot make it back, because most of a person’s daily calorie burn happens in small constant movement: standing up, pacing, fidgeting, walking down a hall. Mayo Clinic endocrinologist James Levine demonstrated this directly. He put motion sensors on 20 volunteers, half lean and half obese, with similar jobs and lives, and tracked them for ten days. The lean group stood and walked 2. 5 hours more each day than the obese group. That added up to 350 extra calories per day in pure metabolic difference, with no formal exercise involved. The American workday now eliminates almost all of that. The American body burned fewer calories because the economy stopped paying anyone to burn them. Manufacturing went overseas, and service work moved into chairs. The right photo is what the American body looks like after two generations of getting paid to sit.

Part 5. On Christmas Day 1948, a researcher named Thomas Jukes weighed his lab chickens. The ones fed antibiotics had grown one-third larger on an identical diet. That accidental discovery rebuilt American agriculture, and turned out to be doing the same thing to human bodies all along. Jukes was an animal nutritionist at Lederle Laboratories, the New York drug company that made aureomycin. He was investigating whether the antibiotic contained the recently discovered vitamin B12 as a fermentation byproduct, since Merck scientists had shown B12 boosted chick growth. Jukes set up the experiment expecting the antibiotic group to grow about as fast as chicks given pure B12. The Christmas Day weigh-in produced a result Jukes hadn’t been looking for. The antibiotic chicks had grown faster than even the B12 chicks, which meant the antibiotic was promoting growth through some other mechanism. The news traveled fast. Within months, drug companies were running trials on cattle, pigs, and turkeys; every species gained more weight on less feed when given low-dose antibiotics. In 1951, the FDA approved aureomycin and five other antibiotics for use in animal feed. There was no public notice and no hearing. The Federal Security Administrator said it would “be against public interest to delay. ” Modern factory farming was born. For decades, no one understood the mechanism. The leading guess was that antibiotics killed off chronic infections that stole calories from the animal. Then the human microbiome research caught up. In 2012, Martin Blaser’s lab at NYU showed in mice what farmers had been doing to chickens for sixty years: low-dose antibiotics given in early life made the mice fatter for life. The mechanism: certain gut bacteria are very good at extracting calories from food, and antibiotics kill off the diverse mix while letting those calorie-extracting strains take over. The mice ate the same amount as controls but pulled more calories from every bite. The same pattern is showing up in human data. Antibiotic prescriptions for American children in the first two years of life have grown enormously since the 1950s, and exposure to multiple courses by toddlerhood is now standard. Studies have linked early-life antibiotic exposure to higher rates of childhood obesity. Children born by C-section, who miss the first dose of maternal microbiome from the birth canal, also show higher obesity rates later. About 70% of antibiotics sold in the United States today still go to livestock. The same drugs that turned a 1948 lab chicken into a one-third-larger bird are still in the food supply, and now in most people’s medical history. The 1948 chicken experiment was about making meat cheaper. It set off one of the largest unintended changes to human metabolic biology in the modern era. The right photo is what happens when three generations of human guts have grown more efficient at pulling calories from food.
